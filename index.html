What LonelyLessAustralia does

LonelyLessAustralia helps policy makers and partners explore options for investing in programmes that reduce loneliness among older adults. It links three main inputs:

  • Preferences and willingness-to-pay estimates from a discrete choice experiment with older adults and key stakeholders.
  • Costing templates for loneliness support programmes such as befriending, community groups and guided digital support.
  • Simple wellbeing multipliers that translate programme reach and intensity into loneliness-free years and indicative monetary benefits.

Users can set a programme configuration, see predicted uptake, willingness to pay, costs and benefit–cost ratios, and compare saved scenarios side by side for business case discussions.

The tool is designed to support transparent conversations with governments, service providers and funders. It keeps assumptions visible and connects preference evidence directly to programme design, cost summaries and wellbeing implications.

How to use the tool

  1. Go to the Configuration tab and choose programme type, delivery setting, contact frequency, group size, facilitator, waiting time to first contact, cost per participant per month, group size and number of groups.
  2. Keep the cost slider within the range used in the experiment so that results remain consistent with the underlying models.
  3. Select the preference segment, for example the overall sample or a high-need subgroup.
  4. Click Apply configuration to update all calculations, then use View results summary to open a scenario summary with headline messages.
  5. Save promising scenarios in the Saved scenarios tab and download them as Excel or a policy brief PDF for reporting and funding discussions.
  6. Use the Population simulation and Sensitivity / DCE benefits tabs to explore how results change when scaling to larger populations, using alternative benefit definitions or testing different assumptions in the Advanced and methods tab.

LonelyLessAustralia is designed for laptops, desktops, tablets and mobiles.

Attributes and levels in the loneliness preference study

The discrete choice experiment asked older adults and other stakeholders to choose between alternative loneliness support programmes and an opt-out option. Each programme was described by the attributes below. LonelyLessAustralia keeps programme configurations inside the experimental range so that results remain consistent with the preference models.

Opt-out and preference models

Element Definition How the tool uses it
Opt-out alternative
An option where no new programme is offered for the scenario being considered.
The DCE included an explicit opt-out option. The tool uses this as the benchmark when calculating uptake. Uptake is the predicted share choosing the programme configuration rather than opting out.
Preference models
Overall mixed logit model and latent subgroups such as high-need and more cautious respondents.
Users can switch between segments to see how uptake, willingness-to-pay and benefit–cost ratios differ for groups with different attitudes or levels of loneliness risk. This is useful for targeting and sensitivity analysis.

A separate technical appendix sets out the full model specification, including the alternative specific constant for programme options, the opt-out constant and the way cost and attribute coefficients are converted into willingness-to-pay measures.

Programme uptake and non-uptake

These percentages are obtained by plugging the chosen configuration into the preference model and comparing the utility of the programme with the utility of having no new programme. They summarise how attractive the configuration is to people in the study.

Take up programme -

Estimated share of older adults who would join the programme if it were offered as described.

Do not take up -

Estimated share who would prefer not to join this programme under the assumptions in the study.

Willingness to pay and economic outcomes

Willingness to pay (WTP) per participant per month is derived from the preference model by dividing attribute coefficients by the cost coefficient. It indicates how much value participants attach to the bundle of attributes in this configuration relative to a baseline. Total WTP aggregates this value across all participants over a one-year horizon.

WTP per participant per month -

Indicative value participants place on this configuration per participant per month, relative to the baseline design used in the experiment.

Total WTP (all groups, 1 year) -

WTP per participant per month multiplied by the number of participants reached and months of support.

Programme cost (all groups, 1 year) -

Direct programme cost over one year, including any volunteer time when the toggle is on.

Net benefit (WTP – cost) -

Total willingness-to-pay benefit minus programme cost over one year.

Benefit–cost ratio (WTP only) -

Total WTP benefit divided by total cost. Values above one suggest that participants’ valuation of the programme exceeds its cost on the assumptions used.

Social and wellbeing outcomes

Participants reached are obtained by combining predicted uptake with group size, number of groups and a completion rate assumption. Loneliness-free years are derived from the number of participants reached, a per-participant effect and the planning horizon set in the Advanced and methods tab.

Participants receiving support (per year) -

Estimated number of older adults who actively participate in the programme in a typical year.

Loneliness-free years (per year) -

Approximate number of loneliness-free years generated each year, based on assumptions in the Advanced and methods tab.

Indicative monetary value of loneliness-free years (per year) -

Loneliness-free years multiplied by the value per loneliness-free year specified in the Advanced and methods tab.

Cost profiles and assumptions

Cost profiles allocate total programme cost across categories such as staff and facilitator time, venues, training, transport and overheads. These percentages can be refined as more detailed costing information becomes available from service providers and commissioning agencies.

When the volunteer time toggle is on, an additional share of cost is attributed to the value of unpaid volunteer input. When it is off, the cost breakdown focuses on paid financial costs only.

About LonelyLessAustralia

LonelyLessAustralia is part of a broader research programme on loneliness and social connection among older adults. It is designed to provide a structured way to test programme options and explain uptake, cost and willingness-to-pay implications in policy discussions.

The tool is intended to support governments, health services, community organisations and funders as they plan investments in loneliness support. It helps answer questions such as:

  • How do costs and benefits change when moving from monthly to weekly contact?
  • What happens to uptake when programmes are delivered at home rather than in community venues?
  • How many groups and participants need to be supported to reach a meaningful share of the at-risk population?

Prepared by Mesfin Genie, PhD, Newcastle Business School, The University of Newcastle, Australia. For questions or clarifications please contact mesfin.genie@newcastle.edu.au.

Advanced settings and assumptions

This panel allows technical users to adjust key multipliers for workshops without editing code. Changes affect calculations immediately in this browser session and are reflected in the Results, Population simulation and Sensitivity / DCE benefits tabs.

Use these settings when you want to align assumptions with national estimates or with partner discussions. For example, you can change the indicative value per loneliness-free year, the completion rate, the size of the at-risk population or the AUD to USD display rate.

Common choices are between 1 and 5 years.
