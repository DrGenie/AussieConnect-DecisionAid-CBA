LonelyLessAustralia Decision Aid Tool

DCE informed decision support for programmes that reduce loneliness among older adults in Australia.

Government decision support Prototype for policy trials

Purpose of the tool

Loneliness affects a large number of Australians, particularly older adults, and is linked with poorer health, reduced quality of life and higher health system costs. This decision aid uses discrete choice experiment results to predict programme uptake and to value programme features using willingness to pay and health outcomes.

The tool is designed for central and state level policymakers, commissioners, and programme managers who want to test alternative configurations of loneliness support services before making investment decisions.

What you can do

  • Review key attributes and levels that older adults value in loneliness support programmes.
  • Configure a scenario with chosen programme features, costs, state specific adjustments and opportunity costs.
  • View willingness to pay results for each attribute from the discrete choice experiment.
  • Assess predicted uptake for your configuration, relative to opting out.
  • Compare cost benefit results using both QALY based and DCE WTP based benefit measures.
  • Save scenarios, generate briefing text, and export summaries for meetings and reports.
  • Use video overviews and the technical appendix to understand the underlying methods.

How to use this decision aid

  1. Start with the Attributes tab to understand the programme features and reference levels, using the information icons to see definitions.
  2. Move to the Configuration tab. Enter a scenario name and notes, choose programme levels, set the cost per session, select the state, and choose whether to include opportunity costs of participants.
  3. Click Apply configuration to run the model. This updates the configuration at a glance, the headline recommendation, the briefing text, and the costs and benefits calculations.
  4. Use View results summary to see a concise summary of uptake, benefits and costs in a pop up window.
  5. Use Save scenario from the Configuration or Saved Scenarios tab to store the configuration, with uptake and benefit cost indicators, for later comparison and export.
  6. Explore WTP Results, Predicted Uptake, and Costs & Benefits tabs to see charts and tables for each dimension.
  7. Use the Video Overview tab and the Technical Appendix to support communication with colleagues, World Bank teams and ministries.

Key Attributes

The discrete choice experiment included the following programme attributes. Reference categories are shown in the information boxes where relevant. All willingness to pay values in the tool are defined relative to these reference categories.

Reference level is support at home. Alternatives are delivery in the local area (around 12 km) or in a wider community area (more than 50 km away).

Accessibility

Where the programme is offered, ranging from local neighbourhood services to wider community locations.

Reference level is daily contact. Alternatives are weekly or monthly sessions to support longer term engagement.

Frequency of interaction

How often participants meet or connect with the programme, for example weekly or monthly.

Reference level is in person contact. Alternatives are virtual delivery or hybrid models that combine in person and online elements.

Method of interaction

How the support is delivered, including in person, virtual, or hybrid models.

Reference level is a 30 minute interaction. Alternatives include sessions lasting two hours or four hours.

Duration of each interaction

Length of each contact, for example two hour or four hour sessions.

Reference level is peer support. Alternatives include community based social groups, psychological counselling, and virtual reality based engagement.

Type of support programme

Programme focus, such as community engagement activities, psychological support, or virtual reality sessions.

Willingness to pay results

Willingness to pay values are derived from the discrete choice experiment by dividing attribute coefficients by the negative of the cost coefficient. They represent how much, in Australian dollars per session, older adults are willing to pay for changes in programme features, relative to reference categories.

These willingness to pay values are also used in the cost benefit analysis as a DCE based benefit measure, complementing health outcome based valuations.

Predicted programme uptake

The predicted uptake is calculated from the choice model using the configured attribute levels and cost per session, compared with opting out. It reflects the probability that an older adult would enrol in the programme.

Costs and benefits analysis

This tab reports both health outcome based and DCE willingness to pay based benefit measures. Health outcomes are expressed as quality adjusted life years (QALYs), valued at 50,000 Australian dollars per QALY. DCE based benefits are derived from willingness to pay for the configured programme and represent the monetary value that participants place on the package of attributes.

QALY gains are illustrative and can be aligned with external evidence. For example, see: Engel L, Lee Y Y, Le L K D, Lal A, Mihalopoulos C (2021). Reducing loneliness to prevent depression in older adults in Australia: A modelled cost effectiveness analysis. Mental Health and Prevention, 24.

QALY benefits

Health outcome benefits are calculated as the number of participants multiplied by the QALY gain per participant, valued at A$50,000 per QALY. This gives a health sector style valuation of the loneliness programme.

DCE WTP based benefits

DCE based benefits are calculated using the sum of willingness to pay for the selected attributes per participant, multiplied by the expected number of participants. This gives a participant valuation of the configured programme.
