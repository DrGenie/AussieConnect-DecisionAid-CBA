What this tool does

The LonelyLessAustralia Decision Aid Tool is a web-based application that helps policy makers and stakeholders explore different programme designs to reduce loneliness among older adults in Australia. It combines:

  • Estimated preferences from a discrete choice experiment using an Error Components Logit (ECL) model;
  • Predicted programme endorsement (uptake) for different support designs;
  • Cost projections based on out-of-pocket costs per session and a system cost multiplier; and
  • Two complementary benefit metrics: (1) QALY-based monetised benefits and (2) WTP-based benefits derived from the DCE.

The aim is not to provide a single “answer” but to support structured dialogue about which programme features offer the best value for money and align with older adults’ preferences.

How to use the tool

  1. Go to the Program design tab and define one or more programme scenarios by selecting the type of support, mode of interaction, frequency, duration, accessibility and cost per session.
  2. Specify the number of older adults reached per year by each scenario.
  3. Adjust the QALY, value-per-QALY and cost multiplier assumptions in the sidebar to reflect your preferred health economic assumptions.
  4. Review the Results & charts tab to compare annual costs, QALY-based net benefits, WTP-based benefits and benefit–cost ratios across scenarios.
  5. Use the Technical appendix tab to see the underlying DCE estimates, attribute levels, and methodological assumptions.

Perspective and scope

The current version takes a broad economic perspective. Programme costs are proxied using the out-of-pocket cost per session from the DCE, scaled up by a configurable multiplier to reflect wider system resource use. Benefits are valued in two ways:

  • QALY-based benefits – assuming a small annual QALY gain per engaged participant, monetised using a configurable value per QALY; and
  • WTP-based benefits – using the estimated marginal willingness-to-pay for preferred programme features, multiplied by the predicted endorsement rate and sessions delivered.

These should be interpreted as indicative economic values to support prioritisation rather than as definitive cost-effectiveness estimates.

Discrete choice experiment: attributes and levels

The tool is calibrated using an ECL model estimated from a discrete choice experiment on preferences for loneliness support programmes among older adults in Australia. The attributes and levels used in the experiment are summarised below.

Attribute Description Levels
Type of support programme How adults connect with others and what kinds of activities they might do together. Peer support; Community engagement; Psychological counselling; Virtual reality (VR)
Method of engagement or interaction How individuals prefer to connect, including those with mobility limitations or who prefer digital communication. In-person; Virtual; Hybrid (virtual and in-person)
Frequency of interaction / engagement How regularly older adults participate in the programme. Daily; Weekly; Monthly
Duration of each interaction The length of each interaction or activity. 30 minutes; 2 hours; 4 hours
Accessibility / convenience Ease of accessing the programme, including physical mobility and transport. At home; Local area (up to 12 km travel); Wider community travel (50+ km)
Cost of support programme Out-of-pocket cost older adults pay each time they participate in the support programme. Free; AUD 5 per session; AUD 20 per session; AUD 50 per session

From preferences to economic value

The tool uses the preference and WTP estimates in two main ways. First, the alternative specific constants and attribute coefficients are combined with the cost coefficient to estimate the latent utility of each programme scenario relative to an opt-out option. Assuming a standard logit choice rule, this yields a predicted endorsement rate for each scenario among older adults offered the programme.

Second, the marginal WTP figures are used to construct a per-session WTP value for each scenario compared with a reference design (peer support, in-person, daily, 30 minutes, at home). This scenario-level WTP, multiplied by the number of sessions delivered and the number of engaged participants, provides an aggregate WTP-based measure of annual benefits from the participants’ perspective.

In parallel, the tool allows users to specify assumptions about the annual QALY gain achieved by sustained participation in a loneliness support programme and the monetary value per QALY. These QALY-based benefits are monetised and compared with the projected system costs, giving an alternative benefit–cost perspective that aligns with standard health economic evaluation practice.

External evidence and default assumptions

The default health economic assumptions in the tool are deliberately conservative and can be adjusted. A modest QALY gain per engaged participant-year is assumed, reflecting the evidence that loneliness and social isolation are associated with lower health-related quality of life, and that group-based social and psychological interventions can improve social functioning and mental health among older adults, albeit with small to moderate effect sizes on generic quality of life measures.

The default monetary value per QALY is aligned with commonly cited Australian cost-effectiveness thresholds used in reimbursement and guideline discussions. These thresholds are not official “prices” for a QALY but provide a practical range (for example, AUD 40,000–70,000 per QALY) for sensitivity analysis in policy-facing tools.

Users are encouraged to adapt these defaults to match the perspective, time horizon and decision context of their specific policy question, and to interpret the resulting benefit–cost ratios as an input into deliberation rather than a strict decision rule.
